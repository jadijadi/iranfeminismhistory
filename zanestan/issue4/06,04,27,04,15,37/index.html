Urgent Action Alert
Maquila Solidarity Network
April 18, 2006


MSN is sending you this Urgent Action Alert at the request of the Hermosa
workers and the European Clean Clothes Campaign (CCC). Workers at the
Hermosa garment factory in San Salvador, El Salvador, need our solidarity.
Their factory closed in May of last year, leaving 190 workers jobless, and
without payment of outstanding wages and severance pay. Almost a year
later, the situation remains unchanged.

The announcement that the factory was being closed came shortly after the
workers won the legal registration of a union. Prior to the factory
closure, the employer also had a long history of worker rights violations,
particularly concerning payment of wages and social security contributions.
After the factory closed, workers also learned that the owner, Mr. Montalvo,
had failed to pay US$353,000 in premiums for the government social security
and pension funds. The debt dates back from 1996.

What do ex-Hermosa workers want?

1. Workers want to be paid their legally owed severance and pension fund
payments, outstanding wages, and payment for overtime worked since 2004.
The workers' legal representatives estimate that the amount due in
outstanding wages and various other payments (excluding pension funds)
amounts to, at minimum, US$127,823.

2. Workers want to be reinstated at the Hermosa factory if and when it is
reopened, as has been promised by factory management, or at the other
nearby factories that produce for some of the same brands.

Workers and the groups supporting them in El Salvador have exhausted all
legal channels to pressure the factory owner and the Salvadorian
authorities to act responsibly. They are now asking their allies to bring
more pressure on the brands that have been sourcing from Hermosa over the
past years to resolve the case, pay outstanding monies due to the workers,
and ensure that workers are reinstated in other contract facilities. Buyers
at Hermosa have included Adidas, Nike, Russell Athletic, Wal-Mart (White
Stag), Kids Connection, and Speedo.

Please note that a number of the brands have made efforts to encourage the
Salvadoran government to take action to resolve the outstanding issues.
However, their intervention has not resulted in workers receiving the
compensation or job opportunities they deserve. We believe the brands also
have a responsibility to ensure that the workers receive their legal
benefits and employment opportunities in other factories producing for
these companies.

For a short history of and background to the case, and steps taken to date
to engage with the buyers, the factory owner, and the Salvadorian
authorities, visit the CCC website:
www.cleanclothes.org/urgent/06-03-21.htm#his .

*REQUESTED ACTION*

Write a letter to one or all of the brands that have been sourcing from
Hermosa over the past five years. Demand that they do the following:

* Contribute to a fund to cover the workers' legally outstanding wages,
overtime payments and severance pay;
* Take the necessary steps to facilitate the reinstatement of the workers
at the Hermosa factory or at other nearby supply factories they use in El
Salvador; and
* Prevent further retaliation against these workers in the form of
intimidation, blacklisting, or other forms of discrimination in hiring.

*SAMPLE LETTER *

Dear [add name]

I'm writing to express my serious concern regarding the situation of the
workers at the Hermosa factory in El Salvador that in recent years produced
clothes for your company. I have been informed by the Clean Clothes Campaign
(CCC) and the Maquila Solidarity Network (MSN) that these workers lost their
jobs in May 2005, and since then have been campaigning to be reinstated and
to receive their legally due severance pay and outstanding wages.

Obviously, the workers are distressed by their difficult situation and now
face great hardship due to their loss of employment. Many of them are
reportedly being punished for defending their rights and are being
discriminated against when they apply for other employment. It would appear
that they are being blacklisted.

I understand that efforts have been made by a number of buyers to convince
the factory owner and the local governmental authorities to settle the
matter, but that so far these efforts have not been successful. I also
understand that the CCC and other labour rights groups have contacted you
over the past month and asked you to do the following:

* Contribute to a fund to cover the workers' legally outstanding wages,
overtime payments and severance pay;
* Take the necessary steps to either get the factory re-opened, or to get
the workers reinstated at other nearby supply factories used by your
company in El Salvador; and
* Take appropriate action to prevent further retaliation against these
workers, in the form of intimidation, blacklisting, or other forms of
discrimination in hiring.

I am very disappointed to learn that so far no concrete action has been
taken by your company to meet these specific requests.

Reports I have received indicate that the owner of the Hermosa factory has
been violating Salvadoran labour laws, particularly with respect to wages
and social security payments, for a number of years. As well, the factory
was closed shortly after workers achieved the legal recognition of a union,
indicating that their right to freedom of association has also been
violated.

Despite the fact that your company has a code of conduct and a monitoring
program, you failed to identify and address these violations at the time
when the factory was still open. Your company, therefore, must share some
of the responsibility for your supplier's failure to meet these minimum
legal and code standards.

Hermosa workers are now being triply punished: not only did they work for
years in a factory that failed to respect national and international labour
standards, they also lost their jobs because they exercised their
internationally recognized right to organize to improve these conditions,
and they are now being discriminated against when they seek other
employment. Finally, they are being denied their severance pay and
outstanding wages (to which they are legally entitled). Surely the
responsible action on the part of your company would be to meet the
workers' demands outlined above.

Thank you in advance for your prompt reply to my letter. I look forward to
receiving a report on the steps your company is taking to ensure that the
legitimate demands of the Hermosa workers are met without further delay.

Sincerely
